## Context

It all started with a simple observation I made while walking Mel, my dog, in the neighborhood park. It was a Sunday morning, and I noticed something I'd never paid attention to before: within a 500-meter radius, there were **three pet shops**, two veterinary clinics, and at least ten people offering informal dog walking services.

I had already looked for a dog walker a few times myself and always ran into the same problem — WhatsApp groups, friend recommendations, Instagram story posts that disappear in 24 hours. There was no centralized place where I could compare, schedule and pay for the service.

At that moment, I sat on a bench, opened Notes and wrote: **"What if there was a pet services marketplace?"**

## The real problem

Before jumping into coding, I decided to talk to real people. I spent two weeks interviewing pet owners and service providers in my area. The results were enlightening:

| Problem | Pet owners (%) | Providers (%) |
|---------|----------------|---------------|
| Difficulty finding a nearby professional | 78% | — |
| Confusing WhatsApp scheduling | 65% | 72% |
| Last-minute cancellations | 43% | 38% |
| No quality guarantee | 57% | — |
| Difficulty finding new clients | — | 81% |
| Default/non-payment | — | 29% |

What caught my attention was the asymmetry: owners complained about **not finding** trustworthy professionals, while providers complained about **not getting** new clients. Two sides of the same problem — but no one had built the bridge.

```python
# Quick simulation I ran to understand the market

donos = 1000  # sample
prestadores = 200

# Owners who looked for a pet service in the last 3 months
donos_ativos = donos * 0.73  # 73%

# Providers with idle time > 10h/week
ociosos = prestadores * 0.64  # 64%

gap = (donos_ativos * 3) / (ociosos * 10)  # demand vs supply
print(f"Demand/supply ratio: {gap:.1f}x")
# → 3.4x — each professional could serve 3x more
```

The numbers showed something obvious: it wasn't a lack of market. It was a lack of **matching infrastructure**.

## The first version: just a paper prototype

Before any line of code, I drew the flow in a notebook. The original idea was lean:

```
[Provider] → Sign up → Offer service → Set price and area
[Owner] → Search → Filter by distance → Schedule → Pay
[System] → Notify both parties → Confirm → Release payment
```

It seems simple today, but at the time I spent a week refining this flow. One early decision that proved correct: **payment only after service completion confirmation**. No charging upfront. This built trust on both sides.

```python
# Payment logic defined in the prototype
class Servico:
    def __init__(self, prestador, dono, valor, data):
        self.prestador = prestador
        self.dono = dono
        self.valor = valor
        self.data = data
        self.status = "pendente"  # pendente → confirmado → realizado → pago

    def confirmar(self):
        """Provider confirms availability"""
        self.status = "confirmado"
        # Notifies owner

    def realizar(self):
        """Service was completed"""
        self.status = "realizado"
        # Releases payment after 24h

    def pagar(self):
        """Releases the amount to the provider"""
        if self.status == "realizado":
            self.status = "pago"
            return True
        return False
```

## The reality check: pricing

When I started researching pricing, the first shock hit. The pet services market in Brazil is **huge but fragmented**:

| Service | Average price (SP) | Provider margin | Market commission |
|---------|-------------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Dog walking (1h) | R$ 35-50 | 85% | 10-15% |
| Bath and grooming | R$ 60-120 | 60% | 10-20% |
| Pet hotel (daily) | R$ 50-100 | 70% | 15-20% |
| Training (session) | R$ 80-150 | 90% | 10-15% |
| Group walk | R$ 25-40 | 80% | 10-15% |

With an average commission of 12%, each R$ 40 transaction generated R$ 4.80 in revenue. To sustain the business with R$ 10k/month in gross revenue, we'd need **~2,100 transactions/month**. Not trivial, but not impossible either — especially considering the Brazilian pet market moves R$ 60 billion/year.

## The learning curve nobody talks about

The first 30 days of ideation were intense:

```
Week 1: Market research and interviews
Week 2: Flow prototype + validation with 5 friends
Week 3: Competitor benchmarking (PetLove, DogHero, local competitors)
Week 4: MVP definition + tech stack
```

The biggest lesson from this phase was: **having the prettiest platform in the world is useless if you don't solve the trust problem.** People trust their pets — which are family members — to strangers. This requires:

1. **Identity verification** — ID, photo, social media
2. **Cross-rating** — owner rates provider and vice versa
3. **Guarantee** — insurance against accidents during service
4. **History** — how many services, how long, reputation

## The competitors I studied

I mapped 5 direct and indirect competitors:

| Competitor | Model | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|------------|-------|-----------|------------|
| DogHero | Boarding | Strong brand, investment | Hotel only, no standalone services |
| PetLove | E-commerce | Logistics, assortment | No service marketplace |
| Guiavet | Directory | Free, many professionals | No scheduling/payment |
| Uber for pets (US) | On-demand | Scale, UX | Outside Brazil, no localization |
| WhatsApp Groups | Informal | Free, local reach | Zero trust, no guarantee |

None of them did **exactly** what I wanted: a full marketplace with scheduling, integrated payment, verification and geolocation.

## What I learned

### 1. The problem really exists — it's not made up

In all 20 interviews I conducted, people confirmed the pain point. I didn't have to convince anyone the problem existed. That was the biggest green flag.

### 2. Pet market is not a niche — it's a continent

R$ 60 billion/year in Brazil, growing 5-7% annually. 150 million pets. 67% of Brazilian households have at least one pet. This isn't a niche — it's infrastructure that's missing.

### 3. Trust is the product, not scheduling

The differentiator won't be having the best search system or the prettiest calendar. It will be **solving trust** — verifying who's on the other side, guaranteeing the service happens, having a dispute channel.

### 4. Starting local is strategy, not limitation

Instead of trying to be the pet services marketplace for all of Brazil, I decided to start in my area (neighborhood, then city). Service marketplaces are local by nature — nobody crosses town to walk their dog.

### 5. The name Dogwalk came from a 5-minute brainstorm

After a terrible list ("PetMatch", "AuAuGO", "PetService Pro", "DogIN"), the name Dogwalk just stuck. It was short, descriptive, easy to remember. The domain `seu.pet` was pure luck — I got it at auction for a ridiculously low price.
